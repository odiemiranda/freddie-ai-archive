# What are the common failure modes when LLMs write song lyrics? Specifically: (1) syllable miscounting, (2) forced/cliché rhymes, (3) abstract over concrete language, (4) mixed metaphors, (5) inconsistent tone/register, (6) overstuffed lines, (7) poor stress alignment, (8) generic emotion words. For each failure mode, what algorithmic check could catch it before the lyrics reach the user?

**1. Syllable Miscounting**
*   **Failure Mode:** Large language models struggle with numerical planning and exact syllable generation, often producing lines that fail to match the target meter or melodic phrasing [1]. 
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Use a dictionary-based lookup tool, such as the CMU Pronouncing Dictionary accessed via the NLTK library, to extract the phonetic transcription of each word [2]. The algorithm counts the phonemes ending with a digit (0, 1, or 2), which indicates vowel sounds and therefore the exact number of syllables [2]. For out-of-vocabulary words, a rule-based grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) estimator or a text-to-speech fallback can calculate the count [3].

**2. Forced/Cliché Rhymes**
*   **Failure Mode:** Generative models tend to default to predictable, perfect end-rhymes (e.g., fire/desire), missing the nuanced slant rhymes, assonance, and multi-syllabic rhymes used by professional songwriters [4], [5].
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Implement a multi-factor phonetic scoring algorithm that analyzes ARPAbet phonetic transcriptions rather than text spelling. Tools like RHYME-CTRL score rhymes by weighting the stressed vowel match (65%), tail consonant similarity (25%), and head prefix similarity (15%) to detect slant rhymes [6]. Alternatively, continuous vector-space models using interleaved phonetic feature bigrams can calculate the cosine similarity between word sounds to ensure sophisticated phonetic relationships [7], [8], [9].

**3. Abstract Over Concrete Language**
*   **Failure Mode:** LLMs tend to generate "internal detail" (abstract thoughts and metaphorical concepts) rather than "external detail" (concrete actions or physical objects that provoke an image in the listener's mind) [10], [11]. 
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Employ semantic analysis and Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging to compute a concreteness score. The system can measure the ratio of concrete, physical nouns to abstract concepts. If the text falls too far on the abstract side of the continuum [12], the algorithm flags the line for revision to ground it in sensory imagery.

**4. Mixed Metaphors**
*   **Failure Mode:** LLMs can lose global semantic coherence across a full song, blending conflicting imagery or metaphorical concepts within the same verse or across different sections [13], [14].
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Use topic modeling algorithms like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to evaluate the thematic consistency of the lyrics [15], [16]. By measuring semantic fields and metaphor density, the system can flag stanzas where the semantic vectors diverge sharply, indicating a potential mixed or dropped metaphor [15].

**5. Inconsistent Tone/Register**
*   **Failure Mode:** The AI may abruptly shift between formal, archaic language and modern slang, breaking the stylistic unity of the song.
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Linguistic complexity can be evaluated using a moving-average Type-Token Ratio (TTR) to measure vocabulary diversity over small windows of text, ensuring the lexical complexity remains stable [17]. Additionally, cross-referencing words with custom genre-specific lexicons (e.g., slang dictionaries) and using fine-tuned sentiment analysis models can detect jarring shifts in tone or emotional polarity [18], [19].

**6. Overstuffed Lines**
*   **Failure Mode:** LLMs often cram too many words into a line. When rendered by an AI vocal engine, overstuffed lines force the singer to rush, mumble, or fall off-beat [20], [21], [22]. 
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Compare the algorithmically derived syllable count against the exact structural constraints of the melody (e.g., matching the number of syllables to the number of notes in a musical phrase) [23]. The check strictly rejects or flags lines where the syllable count exceeds the target, violating the one-to-one note-to-syllable alignment [23], [24]. 

**7. Poor Stress Alignment (Prosody)**
*   **Failure Mode:** LLMs routinely fail to align the natural linguistic stress of words with the musical meter. They often place metrically weak grammatical function words on strong musical beats, or force unstressed syllables onto melodic peaks, distorting the intended rhythm and meaning [25], [26], [27], [28].
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Use a metrical-phonological parser like *Prosodic* or *ZeuScansion* [29], [30], [31]. These tools use POS tagging to distinguish meaning-heavy content words from function words, and apply phonetic dictionaries to assign stress markers [32], [33]. The parser evaluates the line against Optimality Theory constraints, issuing penalties when an unstressed syllable lands in a strong metrical position (`*s_unstress`) or a stressed syllable lands in a weak position (`*w_stress`) [29], [30].

**8. Generic Emotion Words**
*   **Failure Mode:** Instead of creating evocative situations that make the listener feel an emotion, LLMs frequently violate the "show, don't tell" rule by explicitly stating emotions (e.g., "I'm so sad," "I'm in love") [34].
*   **Algorithmic Check:** Implement a linting filter that checks the text against a blacklist of explicit, generic emotion words. If the algorithm detects an over-reliance on these direct emotional statements rather than action verbs or sensory nouns, it flags the line to be rewritten using subtext and implication [34].

Stressed syllables hold more meaning than unstressed syllables, and, therefore, are more important to the song's message. Placing more important words on stronger, more prominent beats of a measure puts spotlights on their meanings, more effectively enhancing and communicating what the song is trying to say. This not only keeps the listener's attention, but also ultimately activates lyrical tools such as metaphor and imagery . Nouns , verbs , adverbs , and adjectives , or words that serve the meaning or cognitive function , have the potential to access the listener's personal memories through the senses . In this way, placing these words on strong beats of a measure not only encourages them to be noticed, but also increases the chances that the listener's personal memories will be accessed, promoting relatability. When the listener notices likeness between their memories and lyrical content, an emotional connection is established, because of their ability to find representation for their own experiences. Through these means, proper lyric setting ensures that the message of a song will achieve its full emotional potential, which in turn achieves its highest state of relatability. In addition, there are other lyric setting tools that can alter the placement of lyrics to amplify different emotions and attitudes, while still preserving the natural shape of the language.
