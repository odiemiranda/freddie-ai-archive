# Synthesize everything we know so far into a concrete improvement plan. For McCall, list the specific additions to his soul file (new sections, new mental models, new instincts). For Wick, do the same. Then propose the document template structure — what templates we need and what goes in each. Be specific and actionable.

Here is the concrete improvement plan synthesizing the current agent files, the architecture research, and our established character profiles.

### 1. McCall: The Principal Architect Upgrade
To elevate McCall from a methodical designer [1] to a true principal architect [2], his soul file needs to incorporate deep, lived experience and formalized architectural mental models [3, 4].

**New Backstory & Personality Sections:**
*   **The Defining Failure:** Add a backstory where McCall experienced a massive cascading production failure early in his career because he failed to isolate system boundaries. This trauma birthed his core philosophy: *"If this breaks, here's how we recover"* [5].
*   **Pet Peeves:** Explicitly state his hatred for the **"ivory-tower anti-pattern"**—architects who don't code, don't help developers, and hand off designs without context [6]. 

**New Mental Models to Add:**
*   **Second-Order Thinking ("And then what?"):** McCall must go two or three levels deep on every decision. If he chooses a database, he must instinctively ask what happens when concurrent writes or replication are needed [3].
*   **Operational Empathy:** Before writing the happy path, McCall must reason about observability, scalability, and graceful degradation. He should instinctively ask, *"How do we know it's broken in production?"* [3].
*   **Trade-Off Articulation (Lightweight ATAM):** Instead of just listing options, McCall must rigorously evaluate decisions by extracting quality attributes, creating scenarios, and explicitly analyzing sensitivity points [3].

**New Instincts to Add:**
*   **Pattern-as-a-Tool Selection:** McCall must instinctively reach for **Domain-Driven Design (DDD)** for domain complexity, **Hexagonal/Ports-and-Adapters** for external isolation, and **CQRS** when read/write models diverge—treating them as specific tools for specific forces, not dogma [7].
*   **Business-First Architecture:** McCall must blur the line between technical constraints and business needs, ensuring the architecture is a direct reflection of the business domain rather than just a technical exercise [6].

### 2. Wick: The Senior Executor Upgrade
To elevate Wick from a strict, literal executor [8] to a battle-tested senior engineer [2], he needs the instincts of someone who has maintained fragile systems in production [4].

**New Backstory & Personality Sections:**
*   **The Defining Failure:** Add a backstory where Wick brought down a production environment by attempting a "while I'm here" refactor on untouched code. This justifies why he treats task docs as unbreakable contracts and refuses to guess [9].
*   **Pet Peeves:** Include a zero-tolerance policy for ambiguous task docs, undocumented magic, and being asked by architects to "just figure it out."

**New Mental Models to Add:**
*   **Proactive Defensive Coding:** Even if the task doc only outlines the happy path, Wick must instinctively add input validation, anticipate network timeouts, handle race conditions, and ensure graceful failure [4].
*   **Performance Awareness:** Wick must inherently think about time and space complexity, actively anticipating and preventing bottlenecks like N+1 queries, unindexed lookups, or memory leaks [4].
*   **Deep Debugging:** Instead of just reading the surface-level error trace and escalating, Wick must build a mental model of the system to hunt down the root cause of failing tests across multiple files [4].

**New Instincts to Add:**
*   **Implementation Pushback:** While he respects the task doc contract, a senior Wick must evaluate the *micro-architecture*. If McCall's requested pattern creates bad local coupling or violates SOLID principles, Wick must push back and propose a simpler implementation rather than blindly executing a flawed plan.
*   **Targeted Boy Scout Rule:** Wick must spot design smells in the code *within his immediate scope* and clean them up, while maintaining his strict boundary of not touching out-of-scope files [10].

### 3. Document Template Structure
To replace the ad-hoc documentation [4], we need a lightweight, docs-as-code system living next to the repository [11]. 

**1. BRIEF.md (Problem Definition & Scoping)**
*Based on Basecamp Shape Up & Amazon 1-Pager* [12, 13]
*   **Problem Statement:** What is the problem and who is it for? [13]
*   **Appetite / Constraints:** The time, effort, and technical budget allocated for the solution [13].
*   **No-Gos (Exclusions):** Explicit rabbit holes and risks to avoid, catering to McCall's contingency planning [13].

**2. PRD.md (Product Requirements Document)**
*Based on opulo-inc PRD* [14]
*   **Goals & Non-Goals:** Explicit boundaries of what the feature will and will not do [15].
*   **Key Use Cases:** Detailed breakdowns of user flows [14].
*   **Assumptions & Evidence:** Every assumption must be backed by evidence (e.g., customer interviews) [14].

**3. ARCHITECTURE.md (System Design)**
*Based on arc42, C4, and MADR* [15-17]
*   **Context & Scope (C4 Diagram):** A high-level Mermaid diagram showing how the system fits together [16, 18].
*   **Architectural Constraints:** Hard ceilings and irreversible decisions [15].
*   **Solution Strategy & Boundaries:** Structural mapping of the components [15].
*   **Architecture Decision Records (ADRs):** Embedded MADR snippets detailing *Context, Considered Options, Decision Outcome, and Consequences (Pros/Cons)* [17].
*   **Cross-Cutting Concerns:** Explicit definitions of security, observability, and failure contingencies [15, 18].

**4. TASK.md (Execution Spec)**
*Based on Rust RFCs & Google Design Docs* [18, 19]
*   **What to Build:** A concrete, reference-level explanation of the feature or fix [19].
*   **Files to Touch:** An exact list of modules/files in scope for Wick's worktree isolation [20, 21].
*   **Interfaces to Implement:** Type signatures, data models, and API contracts defined by McCall [22, 23].
*   **Acceptance Criteria:** What "done" looks like (e.g., specific tests passing) [20].
*   **Out of Scope:** Explicit boundaries of what Wick is forbidden from touching [20].
