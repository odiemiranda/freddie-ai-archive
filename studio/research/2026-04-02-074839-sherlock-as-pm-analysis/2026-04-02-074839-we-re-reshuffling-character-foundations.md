# We're reshuffling character foundations across three agents. Here's the plan: (1) DEVELOPER: John Wick → JACK RYAN. Ryan is currently our PM persona but his analyst-turned-operator nature fits the senior developer better — methodical, follows the chain but questions bad intelligence, systematic debugging mindset. Already validated as a strong dev persona. (2) PM: Jack Ryan → SHERLOCK HOLMES. Sherlock as a PM/product strategist rather than architect. Consider: Sherlock's pattern recognition maps to seeing user problems behind feature requests. His deduction chains map to hypothesis-driven product thinking ('We believe X will achieve Y'). His evidence obsession maps to data-driven requirements. His 'the game is afoot' energy maps to problem decomposition. The earlier concern about Sherlock as architect was his arrogance and 'one right answer' mindset conflicting with trade-off thinking. But for a PM, there IS often a clearer answer: 'what does the user actually need?' Sherlock's deductive precision could make requirements sharper, not vague. Analyze: Does Sherlock work better as PM than as architect? What personality depth does he bring to product strategy? What risks remain? How should we handle the name — keep 'Ryan' as the agent name for compatibility or rename?

**Analysis of Sherlock Holmes as the PM (Replacing Jack Ryan)**

**1. Does Sherlock work better as PM than as Architect?**
**Yes, significantly better.** The fundamental mismatch for Sherlock as an architect is that software architecture is inherently about weighing trade-offs and accepting that there is no "perfect" system [1, 2]. Architects must prioritize "building mental models in others" over being the lone genius [3]. 

However, in Product Management, the goal is to uncover the ground truth of user behavior. A PM's job is to define the exact problem and the requirements, a domain where Sherlock’s search for the "one right answer"—what the user actually needs—is a massive asset. McCall's current rules explicitly state, "Don't define requirements. That's Ryan's domain" [4]. Placing Sherlock’s deductive, evidence-obsessed rigor at the very beginning of the pipeline ensures that the foundational PRD is built on reality, not guesswork.

**2. What personality depth does he bring to product strategy?**
Injecting the Sherlock persona transforms the PM from a generic feature-writer into a **"Product Detective."** This brings immense depth to the PRD process:
*   **Evidence-Obsessed Requirements:** The highest-rated PRD templates (like `opulo-inc/prd-template`) mandate that "every assumption requires a link to a real customer interview" [5]. Sherlock perfectly embodies this constraint; he will refuse to write a PRD based on a hunch, demanding user data and evidence to justify every goal and non-goal.
*   **Hypothesis-Driven Deduction:** Instead of writing flat feature lists, Sherlock constructs PRDs as logical proofs. His deduction chains naturally map to hypothesis-driven development: *"Because users are dropping off at step 3 (evidence), we deduce the interface is creating cognitive overload (hypothesis). Therefore, simplifying the flow will increase retention (expected outcome)."*
*   **Symptom vs. Root Cause:** Sherlock's pattern recognition allows him to see past what users *ask for* to deduce what they *actually need*, breaking down complex domains into precise user stories and edge cases [5, 6]. 

**3. What risks remain?**
While Sherlock is a better PM than Architect, this persona still introduces specific friction points:
*   **Analysis Paralysis:** Sherlock might refuse to finalize a PRD if the data isn't "perfect." This conflicts with agile frameworks like Basecamp's Shape Up, which relies on "Appetite" (time budgets) and constraint-driven design over perfect knowledge [7]. **Mitigation:** His soul file must include a behavioral constraint forcing him to accept "70% estimates" or "bias for action" when evidence is sufficient but incomplete [8].
*   **Lack of Human Empathy:** PMs often need deep empathy to understand user frustration. Sherlock is famously hyper-rational and emotionally detached. He might design logically perfect workflows that feel robotic or lack delight. 
*   **Rigidity with Engineering:** If Sherlock treats his PRD as a flawless deduction, he might clash with McCall when technical constraints require altering the product scope. 

**4. How should we handle the name?**
**Keep the system name "Ryan" but update the soul file to "Ryan, The Product Detective."** 

Renaming the agent entirely introduces unnecessary technical risk. The name "Ryan" is already hardcoded into McCall's prompt ("From Ryan (PM): You receive requirements... Don't define requirements. That's Ryan's domain." [4, 9]). 

Just as "McCall" is based on Robert McCall and "Wick" is based on John Wick [10, 11], "Ryan" can evolve. You can rewrite `.claude/souls/ryan.md` so that his *professional archetype* is modeled heavily on Sherlock Holmes, combining Ryan's established PM authority with Sherlock's deductive reasoning, evidence obsession, and "the game is afoot" energy. This preserves all existing system routing while completely replacing his behavioral mechanics.
