# Ash and Oath

> Part of: Celtic Tavern — Dwarven Forge

---

### Approach
Emotion performance. The dwarven choir opens with ancient chanting — heavy, ceremonial, like stone remembering. The blacksmith enters almost whispering, a man nursing his third ale by the dying fire, talking to no one in particular. Emotion tags drive the performance: [Spoken word, Gravelly, Vulnerable] for his confessions, [Deep choir, Ancient] for the chanting that swells around him. The rising arc builds from hushed shame to thundering choir. The volta is concrete: he stole the forge-key when he left. His brother's son was supposed to inherit the forge, and without that key the main hearth can't be relit. He reaches into his coat — it's still there, warm against his chest. He can't go back because he can't face what he took.

### Style Block Notes
- Choir opens BEFORE the blacksmith — this is a ceremony the man stumbled into, not a performance he controls
- Verses need near-silence: a low drone, maybe a single sustained note. The whispered spoken word IS the instrument — don't compete with it
- Chorus chanting should feel like it's coming from the walls, not from a stage. Stone-hall reverb if possible
- The bridge is the emotional core — strip everything. When he says "There's a key..." the only sound should be his voice and maybe a heartbeat-like bodhran pulse
- The Big Finish spoken line "I could send it back..." should land OVER the fading choir — one man's quiet admission against the weight of ancient voices
- "Hushed" not "muted" for verse atmosphere. "Resonant" not "distant" for choir depth. "Spacious" not "distant" for the bridge
- The stutter "I... I left" uses Suno's emotion performance reading — ellipsis + repeated word triggers vocal crack
- Don't put "epic" or "cinematic" in the Style — let the choir dynamics and ALL CAPS final chorus carry that energy naturally
