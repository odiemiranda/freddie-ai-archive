# Smoke and Copper

> Part of: Dwarven Tavern Storm -- Ale and the Road Home (Set 2)

---

## Approach

**Through-composed (no repeated sections)** -- Every section is new. No chorus, no refrain, no safety net. The piece moves the way a night in a tavern actually moves: each moment replaces the last, and by the end you can't quite remember the beginning. The dwarf arrives wet and heavy, settles into the noise of a full room, drinks, watches, thinks about metal and miles, and by the final section the storm is done and he's reaching for his pack. The through-composed structure mirrors the journey itself -- always forward, never circling back. The emotional anchor isn't a repeated lyric; it's the recurring sense-images of smoke, copper, and iron that thread through without repeating exact lines. Spiral arc: the word "home" appears three times, meaning something slightly different each time.

### Lyrics

```
[Intro]
[Fiddle, solo, slow and searching, rain on stone]

[Verse 1]
[Male baritone, warm, storytelling]
Pushed through the door like pushing through a forge mouth
Wet wool and road dust and three weeks of someone else's iron
The room hit me -- warm air, bread smoke,
a dozen conversations I wasn't part of yet

[Verse 2]
[Mandolin enters, tavern murmur underneath]
The barkeep set the tankard down without a word
Copper rim still warm from the last man's grip
I wrapped my hands around it like a tool I recognized
and let the first swallow do what it does

[Verse 3]
[Building gently, bodhrán soft pulse]
There's a woman at the corner table cutting bread
with a knife that someone made too thick --
I can see the grind marks from here
The steel's decent but the bevel's wrong

That's the trouble with this trade --
you can't stop working even when you stop

[Verse 4]
[Fiddle joins, weaving through]
The crowd's getting louder now
Mugs on oak like a slow percussion
Someone's telling a story I've heard in four different towns
with four different endings, all of them lies

I don't mind
There's a kind of music in a room
that doesn't know you're listening

[Verse 5]
[Quieter, stripped to guitar]
My wife makes a sound when she's banking the fire
Not a word -- more like a breath she's been saving
I haven't heard it in forty-one days
I counted wrong once and started over

Home isn't a place, exactly
It's that sound

[Verse 6]
[Tin whistle enters, airy]
The rain's thinning
I can tell because the roof stopped arguing
and the window's showing stars
where an hour ago it showed nothing

The bread woman's gone
The storyteller's asleep in his chair
The barkeep's stacking mugs
like he's building something small and careful

[Verse 7]
[Full arrangement swells, then pulls back]
Right

Pack's by the door where I left it
Coat's no drier but the road won't wait
for comfortable

There's a city past the ridge
and past that city there's a valley
and in that valley there's a forge
with my name scratched into the anvil

[Outro]
[Solo fiddle, fading, footsteps on wet stone]
Home isn't far now
It just feels far
when you're still sitting down
```

### Style Block Notes

Best paired with an evolving Celtic folk arrangement at 114-120 BPM. The through-composed structure demands an arrangement that never settles -- instruments should enter and exit across sections rather than repeating a fixed arrangement. Start with solo fiddle (intro), add acoustic guitar fingerpicked in verse 1, mandolin in verse 2, soft bodhrán in verse 3, fiddle weaving in verse 4. Strip back to guitar for the intimate verse 5. Tin whistle as a distant color in verse 6. Brief full-band swell in verse 7 that immediately pulls back. Outro is solo fiddle fading into footsteps. Male baritone, warm and conversational, not belting -- this is a man thinking out loud in a room full of noise. D Major with moments in A Minor for the quieter internal sections. Weirdness at 50-55% to support the unconventional no-chorus structure. Style Influence at 50% for Celtic character without forcing repeated melodic patterns.
