## Instrument Notes

### Celtic Harp
- **Character**: Warm, resonant arpeggios and flowing melodies. Softer and rounder than pedal harp — naturally intimate
- **Best for**: Melodic leads, gentle chord arpeggios, atmospheric fills
- **Pairs well with**: Soft drums (bodhrán, brush), bass, Rhodes piano — instruments that leave space for the harp's resonance
- **Avoid**: Bright synths, heavy distortion — harp overtones get buried or clash

### Tin Whistle
- **Character**: Bright, airy, immediately recognizable celtic tone. Clear and piercing in the upper register
- **Best for**: Singable melodies, call-and-response lines, morning brightness
- **Pairs well with**: Piano/Rhodes (harmonic foundation), soft drums, warm bass
- **Avoid**: Other high-register instruments (flute, piccolo) — frequency collision

### Bodhrán
- **Character**: Deep, warm frame drum with a natural bounce. Played with a tipper for rhythmic complexity
- **Best for**: Driving celtic grooves, layering with hip hop drums, adding organic pulse
- **Pairs well with**: Boom-bap kicks (complementary frequencies), celtic melody instruments
- **Avoid**: Heavy electronic drums — bodhrán gets lost under 808s

### Sonic Tips
- For morning coffee, keep celtic instruments "warm" and "soft" — avoids pub-session intensity
- Bodhrán + boom-bap creates an organic hybrid groove that sounds surprisingly natural
- Tin whistle needs space — don't crowd it with competing melodies
- Celtic harp arpeggios function like Rhodes chords in a lo-fi context — use one or the other, not both in the same variation
- 88-95 BPM: faster than lo-fi, slower than a reel — the morning groove pocket
