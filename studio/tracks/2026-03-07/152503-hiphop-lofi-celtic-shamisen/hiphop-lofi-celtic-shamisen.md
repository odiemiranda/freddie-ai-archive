## Variation Summary

1. **The One About the Cartographer** — The most classic lo-fi hip hop structure. Dusty boom-bap with Irish harp replacing Rhodes as the sampled chord instrument. Low whistle carries a lazy melody over the top, shamisen plucks between phrases. Strongest head-nod factor.
2. **Half a Map and a Good Feeling** — Harp and shamisen share the melodic space, trading short plucked phrases over a deep pocket groove. Low whistle enters only in the hook as a warm accent. More textured, slightly more complex arrangement.
3. **Pencil Down, Still Nodding** — Most minimal and focus-friendly. Low whistle carries the melody alone over a stripped-back beat. Harp and shamisen appear sparingly as accents. Least distracting, best for deep programming sessions.

All three use B♭ Major at 90 BPM with boom-bap drums. The head-nod comes from the kick-snare pocket, not the melody.

## Production Notes

- The boom-bap kick-snare pattern is what creates the head-nod — it should feel like a pocket you fall into
- Irish harp works as a "sampled chord" instrument — think chopped jazz samples but with organic harp texture
- Low whistle should be breathy and lazy, behind the beat — not leading, just floating
- Shamisen stays muted and occasional — one pluck every 4-8 bars
- Deep warm bass (not sub-heavy) — should be felt more than heard
- Vinyl crackle glues the Celtic instruments into the lo-fi world
- 90 BPM is slightly above classic lo-fi (82-85) — gives more momentum for work/study
- Run each 4-8 times — boom-bap + Celtic is unusual, some outputs will nail it better than others
- If the harp sounds too classical/bright, try "warm plucked harp, jazz voicings" in a re-run
