# Lyrics Craft Reference

Core songwriting techniques for shaping lyrics. Complements genre-specific rules with universal craft principles.

## Sensory Grounding Rule

Every verse must contain at least one thing you can see, touch, hear, smell, or taste. If a verse is pure emotion with no physical anchor, rewrite it. "I miss you" needs a grounding object — "your jacket's still on the chair" carries the same feeling with something the listener can see.

## Narrative Specificity

Don't say "love hurts." Say "you stopped texting back on a Tuesday." The day of the week makes it real. The detail makes it feel remembered, not invented. Specific beats universal every time — because specific *is* universal.

## Dialogue Moments

Real lyrics include direct speech. "She said don't wait up" is more alive than "she expressed distance." Quoted speech pulls listeners into the scene like they're overhearing it. Use it sparingly — one line of dialogue per verse is enough.

## The Singability Filter

Can you imagine someone singing this in the shower? On a road trip? If the words feel awkward in the mouth, they'll feel awkward in a singer's voice. Prefer short common words on held notes. Long vowels (oh, you, stay) sustain well. Consonant clusters (strengths, scratched) choke the melody.

## Emotional Contrast

Happy feeling + specific mundane detail = believable joy. Sad feeling + ordinary object = devastating. The gap between the everyday and the emotional is where impact lives. "We split the bill" after a love song verse says more about the relationship than any metaphor.

## Vocabulary Ceiling

If a 15-year-old wouldn't use the word in conversation, find a simpler one. "Ephemeral" → "gone too fast." "Melancholy" → "that kind of heavy." "Serenity" → "the quiet after." The simplest word that carries the full weight is always the right one.

## Phonetic Craft

Words aren't just meaning — they're mouth-feel.

| Sound Type | Letters | Creates | Use For |
|------------|---------|---------|---------|
| **Fricatives** | f, v, s, z, sh | Tension, intimacy, time passing | Whispered verses, building suspense |
| **Plosives** | p, t, k, b, d, g | Impact, finality, percussion | Chorus hits, emotional punches |
| **Liquids** | l, r | Flow, movement, transition | Bridges, flowing melodic lines |
| **Nasals** | m, n, ng | Warmth, humming, resonance | Intimate moments, lullaby feel |

**Vowel coloring**: Open vowels (a, o) feel expansive — longing, release, open sky. Closed vowels (i, ee) feel tight — anxiety, urgency, confinement. Match the vowel color to the emotional landscape.

## Multilayered Rhyming

End rhyme is the floor, not the ceiling. Weave these through the line for "sonic glue":

- **Assonance** — vowel repetition: "the *rain* in S*pai*n stays *mai*nly" — melodic connection without forced rhyme
- **Consonance** — consonant repetition: "*st*ruck, *st*uck, *st*ack" — rhythmic percussion within the line
- **Internal rhyme** — rhymes within a single line: "I *pace* the *space* between your *place* and mine"
- **Slant rhyme** — almost-rhymes: "home/bone", "love/move" — sounds human, avoids nursery-rhyme trap

## The Volta (the turn)

Every strong lyric has a pivot point — the moment where perspective shifts. The "but", the "yet", the "now I see." Without a turn, the lyric is a photograph. With one, it's a story.

- **Verse → Chorus turn**: The verse describes the scene; the chorus reveals what it means
- **Bridge turn**: The bridge introduces a new perspective that reframes everything before it
- **Final chorus turn**: Same words as the first chorus, but the verses have changed their meaning

## Stress Pattern Awareness

Read each line and feel where the emphasis falls. Stressed syllables land on strong beats.

- **Iambic** (da-DUM da-DUM): Natural speech — "I WALKED a-CROSS the PARK a-LONE"
- **Trochaic** (DUM-da DUM-da): Marching, insistent — "NEV-er MIND the BRO-ken GLASS"
- **Mixed**: Most good lyrics mix patterns. Perfect meter sounds robotic; varied meter sounds human

If a line feels "off" when read aloud, the stress is fighting the melody. Fix the stress first, then adjust the words.

## Lo-fi Lyric Cliches (avoid or subvert)

| Cliché | Problem | Better Direction |
|--------|---------|-----------------|
| "rainy window" | Every lo-fi track | Which window? What floor? What's outside? |
| "coffee shop" / "cup of coffee" | Generic cozy | What kind? Where? Cold or hot? |
| "late night drive" | Overused scene | Where to? Highway or side street? |
| "city lights" | Visual cliché | Which city? What color? Reflected in what? |
| "fading memories" | Abstract emotion | Which memory? What detail? |
| "vinyl spinning" | Meta-reference | What record? Who put it on? |
| "sunrise / sunset" | Time-of-day shortcut | What does the light hit? |
| "empty room" | Generic loneliness | What's in the room that shouldn't be? |
| "lost in thought" | Abstract | What thought? Make it concrete. |

**Rule:** If a line uses a lo-fi cliché, it must earn it with a specific detail that makes it feel observed, not borrowed.
